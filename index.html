Subway Ridership in NYC Decreases as Fares Increase


By Andrea Gonzales
Filed under New York
Check out the GitHub

A set of subway turnstiles.

The NYC subway system is one of the largest in the world. It's one of the oldest public transportation systems and well-known for operating every hour of the day, every day of the year. While it ranks #7 in annual ridership, it is the largest system by stations, with 472 in service today.1

Subway ridership is defined by the MTA as passengers who enter the subway system; this includes passengers who transfer from buses, and excludes NYC Transit Employees and passengers that exit the subway or have an out-of-station transfer.2 The chart below shows the fluctuation in annual ridership from 2011-2016.

Despite the projected population increase, it seems that since 2013, the annual ridership has decreased across all the boroughs (Staten Island has a railway, but isn't considered a part of the NYC subway system as it has no connections to other subway rails).3 There are many factors that may explain the decrease in ridership over the past two years, but one direct correlation can be made between ridership and recent fare hikes. The chart below describes the increase in price over the years (not accounting for inflation).

The MTA has a long way to go to maintain and rebuild its ridership. NYC went through the aptly-named "Summer of Hell" as the MTA performed eight weeks of emergency track repairs after Governor Cuomo declared a State of Emergency this summer.4 Hopefully the repairs will be enough to convince riders not to find alternative commuting options.
